Comprehensive Stages of Labor

Detailed physiologic and nursing management of the four stages of labor: dilation, expulsion, placental, and recovery. Learn key milestones, maternal changes, and evidence‑based care.

First Stage of Labor (Dilatation)

Begins with onset of true labor contractions and ends with complete cervical dilation (10 cm). Divided into latent, active, and transition phases.
Latent Phase (0–3 cm)
• Contractions mild, 20–40 sec, 5–20 min apart
• Duration: nullipara ~6–20 hrs; multipara ~4.5–14 hrs
• Nursing: encourage ambulation, comfort measures, hydration; avoid early epidural to prevent prolonged labor
Active Phase (4–7 cm)
• Contractions moderate to strong, 40–60 sec, 3–5 min apart
• Cervical dilation accelerates (~1 cm/hr nullipara; 1.5 cm/hr multipara)
• Nursing: pain management options, monitor FHR, assist with breathing techniques
Transition Phase (8–10 cm)
• Contractions peak intensity, 60–90 sec, 2–3 min apart
• May have nausea, vomiting, irritability, trembling legs
• Nursing: avoid pushing until fully dilated; maximal support, prepare for second stage
⚠️ Key assessment: Cervical effacement (thinning) precedes dilation in primiparas; in multiparas both occur together. Monitor for signs of fetal distress.

Second Stage of Labor (Expulsion)

Definition: From full cervical dilation (10 cm) to delivery of the infant.
Duration: Nullipara: up to 3 hours; Multipara: up to 2 hours (with regional anesthesia, up to 3/2 hours).
Physiologic changes: Involuntary bearing‑down urge (Ferguson reflex) due to fetal head on sacral nerves; maternal HR and BP peak during pushing.
Phases of Second Stage:
• Latent (resting): No urge to push; descent begins.
• Active (pushing): Strong urge; directed pushing.
• Descent: Fetal station progresses.
• Crowning: Fetal scalp visible at perineum.
• Birth: Delivery of head, restitution, shoulders, body.
  • Nursing interventions: Position changes (upright, lateral, squatting), encourage open‑glottis pushing, perineal support, warm compresses, monitor FHR after each contraction.
  • Signs of imminent birth: Bulging perineum, separation of labia, crowning, increased bloody show, mother feels "like a bowel movement".
🚨 Emergencies in second stage: Shoulder dystocia, prolapsed cord, fetal distress – prepare for rapid intervention.

Third Stage of Labor (Placental)

Definition: From birth of infant to delivery of placenta.
Duration: 5–15 minutes (prolonged if >30 min).
Mechanism of separation: Uterine contraction shears placenta from decidua; signs: cord lengthens, sudden gush of blood, uterine fundus rises and becomes firm.
Placental delivery methods:
• Schultze (shiny): Fetal surface presents first – more common.
• Duncan (dirty): Maternal surface presents – more bleeding.
• Active management: Oxytocin administration, controlled cord traction (Brandt‑Andrews), uterine massage.
  • Nursing care: Assess vital signs, monitor for excessive bleeding (>500 mL), inspect placenta for completeness, assess fundal tone.
  • Complications: Retained placenta, uterine inversion, postpartum hemorrhage.

Fourth Stage of Labor (Immediate Postpartum)

Definition: First 1–4 hours after placental delivery.
Critical period for hemorrhage detection and stabilization.
Maternal physiologic changes: Fundus remains firm, lochia rubra moderate, vital signs stable or slight bradycardia, afterpains common.
Nursing priorities:
• Fundal massage every 15 min first hour
• Assess lochia (color, amount, clots)
• Monitor vital signs q15min x 1h, then q30min x 2h
• Encourage bonding and breastfeeding (oxytocin release)
• Assist with perineal care, ice packs, pain management
  • Warning signs: Boggy uterus, excessive bleeding (>1 pad/hour), large clots, tachycardia, hypotension, perineal hematoma.
  • Patient education: Report heavy bleeding, fever, severe pain; schedule postpartum follow‑up.

Nursing Role & Interventions Across Stages

  • First stage: Assess cervical dilation/effacement, FHR and contraction pattern, maternal VS; provide comfort (position changes, hydrotherapy, breathing); administer pain relief as ordered; encourage voiding q2h.
  • Second stage: Coach pushing when fully dilated; assist with positioning; provide perineal support; prepare for delivery; monitor FHR after each contraction; anticipate episiotomy if needed.
  • Third stage: Administer oxytocin; assess placental separation signs; perform controlled cord traction; inspect placenta; evaluate fundal tone; monitor for hemorrhage.
  • Fourth stage: Frequent fundal and lochia assessment; promote early skin‑to‑skin contact and breastfeeding; monitor for bladder distention; provide perineal care; document vitals, blood loss.
📌 Key equipment readiness: Newborn warmer, resuscitation equipment, oxytocin, sterile delivery pack, suture materials, hemorrhage kit.
